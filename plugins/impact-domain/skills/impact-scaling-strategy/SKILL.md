# Impact Scaling Strategy

## Overview

This skill helps impact entrepreneurs select and apply the right scaling strategy for
their organisation. Based on the framework developed by the Sociale InnovatieFabriek
(SIF) and described in *Leaving a Legacy* by Kaat Peeters.

The core insight: **more customers ≠ more impact**. Social return doesn't grow the same
way as financial return. An impact-first organisation must choose its scaling path
deliberately.

---

## The Four Scaling Strategies

### 1. Scaling Deep
**Definition:** Deepen the transformation within your existing community rather than
expanding to new people.

**Key characteristics:**
- Focus on quality and depth of impact over breadth
- Strengthen relationships with current beneficiaries
- Invest in lasting, systemic change within a defined scope
- Impact is measured by depth of transformation, not number of people reached

**When it fits:**
- The problem requires deep trust and long-term engagement (e.g. mental health, trauma, care)
- Replication would dilute quality
- The org's moat is relationships, not systems or IP
- Current beneficiaries are not yet maximally served

**Belgian case study:** Blommm — chose scaling deep to maintain the quality of their
model rather than risking dilution through rapid expansion.

**Watch out for:** Staying small can become an excuse for avoiding hard growth questions.
Scaling deep is a deliberate strategic choice, not a default.

---

### 2. Scaling Up
**Definition:** Grow the reach of your impact model — more people, more regions, more
revenue, while preserving the core impact logic.

**Key characteristics:**
- Classic growth: more customers, more volume, more geographic spread
- Requires robust systems, processes, and team capacity
- The model must be replicable at scale without losing impact quality
- Impact is measured by number of people reached and outcomes maintained

**When it fits:**
- The model is proven and systematised
- There is unmet demand that the org can serve
- The impact doesn't depend on founder-level relationships
- Unit economics improve or hold at scale

**Belgian case study:** Welcome To My Garden — used scaling up to reach more people
with their community gardening model while maintaining impact quality.

**Watch out for:** The "growth trap" — scaling up revenue without scaling impact.
Always track impact KPIs alongside financial ones.

---

### 3. Scaling Out
**Definition:** Spread your model by enabling others to replicate it — through
franchising, licensing, open-sourcing, or partnership.

**Key characteristics:**
- The org acts as a knowledge/model provider, not direct service deliverer
- Others implement the model in their own context
- Requires clear documentation, training, and quality control
- Impact is multiplied through the ecosystem, not the organisation itself

**When it fits:**
- The model is highly replicable across different contexts
- Org lacks capacity to grow directly, but the need is widespread
- The org's impact is in spreading a methodology or system
- Partners or franchisees can maintain impact fidelity

**Belgian case study:** OHNE (sustainable supermarket) — used scaling out to spread
their model for zero-waste shopping through partner locations.

**Watch out for:** Quality drift. Without strong training and monitoring, replicators
may implement a diluted version of the model.

---

### 4. Scaling Down
**Definition:** Deliberately reduce scope, size, or complexity to sharpen impact and
improve sustainability.

**Key characteristics:**
- Counter-intuitive but powerful: less can mean more impact
- Eliminate activities that don't serve the core mission
- Can involve geographic focus, narrower target group, or simplified offer
- Impact is measured by focus and effectiveness, not scale

**When it fits:**
- The org is spread too thin across too many activities
- Mission drift has occurred — doing things that don't serve the core goal
- Financial sustainability requires a tighter model
- The organisation is burning out trying to do too much

**Belgian case study:** Tiptoh — changed strategy over the years, choosing to scale down
and focus on their core offering after experiencing mission drift.

**Watch out for:** This is not giving up. Scaling down is a strategic move to build a
stronger foundation. Communicate it as such to stakeholders.

## How to Use This Skill

### Step 1: Diagnose before advising

Ask the user about their organisation before jumping to a recommendation:
1. What social/environmental problem do you solve?
2. Who are your primary beneficiaries, and how many do you currently reach?
3. What does "more impact" mean to you — more people, deeper change, or wider
   adoption of your model?
4. What is your organisation's main bottleneck right now?
5. What would you *not* be willing to sacrifice to grow?

### Step 2: Match to strategy

Use the answers to identify which strategy (or combination) fits best:

| If the org says... | Consider... |
|---|---|
| "We want to reach more people" | Scaling Up or Scaling Out |
| "We want to go deeper with existing community" | Scaling Deep |
| "We want others to use our model" | Scaling Out |
| "We're doing too much and losing focus" | Scaling Down |
| "Our relationships are what make us special" | Scaling Deep |
| "Our model is proven and documented" | Scaling Up or Scaling Out |
| "We're struggling financially despite growth" | Scaling Down first |

### Step 3: Address the trade-offs

Every strategy has trade-offs. Always surface them:
- Scaling up: requires capital, systems, team — can you sustain quality?
- Scaling deep: intentionally small — how do you fund this?
- Scaling out: you lose control — how do you maintain fidelity?
- Scaling down: requires courage to say no — who needs to be convinced?

### Step 4: Link to impact measurement

Remind the user that scaling strategy determines what you measure:
- Deep → depth of change per beneficiary
- Up → number reached × outcome quality
- Out → number of replicators × fidelity score
- Down → sustainability + focus score

## Key Principles

- **Impact-first ≠ growth-first.** Challenge the assumption that bigger is always better.
- **There is no "best" strategy.** The right choice depends on context, mission, and
  the nature of the problem.
- **Strategies can combine.** An org might scale up in one region while scaling deep
  in another. Scaling down often precedes a healthier scaling up.
- **Revisit regularly.** The right strategy at 10 beneficiaries may be wrong at 1000.
