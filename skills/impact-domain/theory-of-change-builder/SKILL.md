# Theory of Change Builder

## Overview

A Theory of Change (ToC) is a strategic map that shows *how and why* an organisation
believes its work leads to meaningful social or environmental change. It connects
activities to outcomes to long-term impact — making the logic explicit and testable.

This skill guides agents through co-building a ToC with impact organisations, from
first principles to a structured, communicable model.

Based on Module 8 of the Sociale InnovatieFabriek's *Sociale Innovatie Atelier*.

---

## Why Theory of Change Matters

- Makes impact logic explicit and challengeable
- Aligns team, board, and funders on what "success" means
- Enables meaningful impact measurement (you can only measure what you've defined)
- Supports grant applications, investor pitches, and partner conversations
- Reveals assumptions that need testing

Common failure modes without a ToC:
- Measuring outputs (# events held) instead of outcomes (# people who changed behaviour)
- Funding activities that feel good but don't connect to impact
- Internal confusion about what the organisation is really trying to achieve

---

## The ToC Framework

A Theory of Change flows left to right:

```
PROBLEM → INPUTS → ACTIVITIES → OUTPUTS → OUTCOMES → IMPACT
             ↑                                          ↑
         (resources                              (long-term,
          you invest)                             systemic change)
```

With **assumptions** sitting beneath every arrow — the beliefs that must hold for each
step to lead to the next.

### Layer Definitions

| Layer | Question it answers | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Problem** | What root cause are we addressing? | Isolation among elderly in urban areas |
| **Inputs** | What do we invest? | Staff time, funding, volunteer network, venue |
| **Activities** | What do we do? | Weekly community lunches, befriending program, skill workshops |
| **Outputs** | What is directly produced? | 200 lunches hosted, 150 matched befriending pairs |
| **Outcomes** | What changes for beneficiaries? | Reduced loneliness, new social connections, increased wellbeing |
| **Impact** | What long-term systemic change results? | Elderly people fully integrated in urban social fabric |
| **Assumptions** | What must be true for each step to work? | Elderly have transport access; volunteers are consistent; etc. |

## How to Build a ToC (Step by Step)

### Step 1: Define the Problem

Start from the *root cause*, not the symptom.

Ask:
- What is the core social or environmental problem you're solving?
- Who experiences this problem, and why does it exist?
- What would happen if your organisation didn't exist?
- What is the *root cause* vs. the visible symptom?

> Tip: Push past "there's not enough X" answers. Why is there not enough X? That's
> usually where the real problem lives.

Output: 1–2 sentence problem statement focused on root cause.

---

### Step 2: Define the Long-Term Impact Vision

Work *backwards* from the world you want to see.

Ask:
- In 10–20 years, if your work succeeds, what has changed in the world?
- What systemic shift would make your organisation unnecessary?
- What would the people you serve say about their lives?

Output: 1 sentence impact vision. Bold, specific, measurable in principle.

> Example: "By 2035, every young person in Flanders has at least one trusted adult
> mentor in their life, reducing school dropout by 40%."

---

### Step 3: Map Outcomes

Work backwards from impact to identify the key behaviour/condition changes needed.

Ask:
- What must change in people's lives for the impact to happen?
- What knowledge, skills, attitudes, or behaviours need to shift?
- What external conditions need to change?

Distinguish:
- **Short-term outcomes** (0–1 year): awareness, knowledge, attitude shifts
- **Medium-term outcomes** (1–3 years): behaviour change, new skills, changed conditions
- **Long-term outcomes** (3–5 years): lasting change in lives, communities, or systems

Output: 3–7 outcome statements, each starting with a verb ("Participants gain...",
"Communities experience...", "Policy shifts to...")

---

### Step 4: Define Activities

Now forward: what activities produce these outcomes?

Ask:
- What specific programmes, services, or interventions do you run?
- Which activities directly lead to which outcomes?
- Are there activities you run that don't connect to any outcome? (flag these)

Output: List of core activities, each linked to 1+ outcome.

---

### Step 5: Identify Inputs

What does the organisation invest to run these activities?

Categories:
- **Financial:** grants, revenue, donations
- **Human:** staff, volunteers, advisors
- **Physical:** space, equipment, materials
- **Social:** network, trust, partnerships
- **Knowledge:** methodologies, expertise, data

Output: Key inputs list. Highlight which are most critical/scarce.

---

### Step 6: Define Outputs

Outputs are the *direct, countable results* of activities — not yet the change.

Ask:
- What can you count at the end of each activity?
- How many people participated? How many sessions ran? How many products delivered?

Output: Measurable output indicators for each activity.

> Common mistake: confusing outputs with outcomes. "100 training sessions delivered"
> is an output. "Participants feel more confident applying for jobs" is an outcome.

---

### Step 7: Surface Assumptions

Every arrow in the ToC rests on assumptions. Make them explicit.

Ask for each link:
- What must be true for this activity to lead to this outcome?
- What could break this connection?
- Which assumptions are you most uncertain about?

Output: 5–10 key assumptions, flagged as HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW certainty.

> These become your learning agenda — what to test first.

---

### Step 8: Identify Risks and Mitigations

Ask:
- Which external factors could undermine your theory?
- What happens if a key assumption proves wrong?
- Who else needs to act for your impact to materialise?

Output: Risk register with mitigation notes.

## Output Format

When complete, present the ToC as a structured summary:

```
THEORY OF CHANGE — [Organisation Name]

PROBLEM:
[Root cause statement]

IMPACT VISION (10+ years):
[Long-term systemic change]

LONG-TERM OUTCOMES (3–5 years):
• ...
• ...

MEDIUM-TERM OUTCOMES (1–3 years):
• ...
• ...

SHORT-TERM OUTCOMES (0–1 year):
• ...
• ...

ACTIVITIES → OUTPUTS:
• [Activity] → [Output]
• [Activity] → [Output]

KEY INPUTS:
• ...

KEY ASSUMPTIONS:
• [HIGH] ...
• [MEDIUM] ...
• [LOW] ...

KEY RISKS:
• ...
```

## Facilitation Tips

- **Don't rush to outputs.** Most orgs jump straight to activities. Spend time on
  problem definition and impact vision first — everything else flows from these.
- **Challenge vague outcomes.** "Increased wellbeing" is not enough. Push for:
  *wellbeing as measured by what, for whom, over what timeframe?*
- **One ToC per programme, not per organisation.** Large orgs often have 3–5 ToCs
  for different programmes. Don't try to fit everything into one model.
- **The ToC is a hypothesis.** Frame it as "we believe..." — it's a living document,
  not a fixed truth.
- **Use it to pick KPIs.** Once the ToC is clear, measurement choices become obvious.
  You measure the outcomes and test the assumptions.
